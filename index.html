The Critical Vulnerability: The "Detection Gap"

The primary roadblock is not physical vandalism itself, but the reactive detection gap. Today, Airtel Nigeria only catches fiber cuts or generator shutdowns when subscribers start experiencing dead zones and flooding the network with complaints. By then, critical downtime has compounded. Our platform targets these vital hours of delay, automating the jump from immediate mechanical failure to technician cell dispatch down to seconds.

Operational Pipeline

The 7-Step Resiliency Pipeline

How data and response orchestrations transition across physical base stations and AWS servers in an event-driven loop.

1
STEP 01 — RECIPIENT FAILURE

Physical Event Occurs

A physical event occurs at an Airtel base station: fiber cable cut, fuel or generator theft, battery drain, or solar equipment tampering.

2
STEP 02 — SENSOR TRANSMISSION

Out-of-Band Upstream

Site sensors capture voltage changes or cut points instantly. The hardware IoT gateway routes this payload to AWS IoT Core over an independent cellular uplink, bypassing the cut line.

3
STEP 03 — THRESHOLD SCAN

CloudWatch Raising Alarm

Amazon CloudWatch receives sensor reports. Upon processing static metric interruptions or anomalous fuel rate drops, it raises an instant alert flag.

4
STEP 04 — SERVERLESS COMPUTING

AWS Lambda Is Triggered

CloudWatch alarms trigger AWS Lambda. Our handler parses telemetry packets, classifies fault categories, and Queries DynamoDB to locate key technical inventory on the site.

5
STEP 05 — DATABASE RECORD

DynamoDB Audit Ledger

DynamoDB logs a permanent, timestamped network incident ledger, documenting exact fault classification, estimated station location, and the assigned repair unit.

6
STEP 06 — NOTIFICATION FEED

SNS Alert Dispatch

Amazon SNS fires target notifications: SMS to regional field response crews, push alerts to security panels in the NOC center, and email updates to command leads.

7
STEP 07 — SECURITY & LOGGING

IAM & Cognito Isolation

AWS IAM enforces strict permissions across microservices, Cognito handles engineer access control, and AWS CloudTrail tracks activity registries for NCC audit reports.

Statement of Work — Section 1

SOW-01: Executive Summary

OFFICIAL CLOUD PROJECT ROADMAP & CAMPUS CAPSTONE STUDY

PROJECT TITLE: Cloud-Driven Infrastructure Resilience and Security for Airtel Nigeria
AUTHOR: Abdus-somad Olanrewaju Gbadamosi (Airtel Africa Tech Fellow)
SPONSOR: AWS Student Builder Group & Airtel Africa Foundation
AFFILIATION: University of Ilorin, Nigeria
STATUS: Final Capstone Release • Q2 2026

Airtel Nigeria, the country's second-largest mobile network operator with over 57 million subscribers, faces a serious and well-documented infrastructure problem. Fibre cables that carry network signals to base stations are being cut at a rate the company itself has confirmed averages 43 incidents every single day. Generator batteries and fuel are being stolen from remote sites. Equipment is being vandalised. Each of these events causes a network outage, and under the current operational model, the company only finds out about them after subscribers have already lost service.

This Statement of Work proposes a cloud-based monitoring and automated alert system built on Amazon Web Services (AWS). The platform uses smart sensors at base station sites to continuously send health data into AWS. When something goes wrong, cloud services detect it instantly, identify what type of problem has occurred, and automatically send alerts to the right field team with everything they need to respond. The entire process takes seconds rather than the hours the current reactive model requires.

CORE PURPOSE

This platform does not attempt to stop vandalism from happening. Its purpose is to make sure Airtel finds out about it within seconds instead of hours, so the gap between physical damage and network restoration becomes as short as possible.

Statement of Work — Section 2

SOW-02: Company Background

2.1 About Airtel Africa Plc
Airtel Africa Plc is one of the largest telecommunications companies on the African continent. It operates across 14 countries in sub-Saharan Africa and is listed on both the Nigerian Stock Exchange (NGX) and the London Stock Exchange (LSE). Its Nigerian subsidiary, Airtel Nigeria, serves over 57 million subscribers across all 36 states of Nigeria and the Federal Capital Territory, providing mobile voice calls, internet data services, and digital financial services via Airtel Money.

2.2 The Nigerian Telecommunications Sector
The Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) reports that the country has over 145,000 active base stations. The digital economy is a major part of Nigeria's overall economy, contributing approximately 18% of GDP in the fourth quarter of 2024. Recognising this importance, President Bola Ahmed Tinubu signed the Critical National Information Infrastructure (CNII) Order on June 24, 2024, classifying telecom structures as critical national assets and making damage to them a serious criminal offence.

2.3 Airtel Nigeria's Competitive Position
In the Nigerian market, Airtel competes directly with MTN Nigeria (approx. 87 million subscribers). Both operate in the same physical threats environment. Because subscribers have Frictionless Mobile Number Portability, network reliability is a direct factor in customer retention; a user experiencing repetitive failures will quickly switch to a competitor.

3.4 The Detection Gap: Where Downtime Really Accumulates
The structural operational bottleneck is that a base station fault is typically discovered only after subscribers start calling to complain. This delayed detection method keeps sites offline for several hours and severely impacts service level agreements.

Statement of Work — Section 4

SOW-04: Problem Statement

The central problem Airtel Nigeria faces is not vandalism itself. Vandalism cannot be solved from a cloud platform. The real problem is that when infrastructure damage occurs at a remote base station, Airtel does not know about it quickly enough to minimise the impact on subscribers. The detection gap between physical failure and operational response is where the majority of subscriber-facing downtime accumulates.

Three Specific Operational Dimensions:

  • Fibre Cable Cuts at Remote Sites: With 43 cuts daily, each cut interrupts signal transmission at the affected site, taking thousands of subscribers offline simultaneously. In rural areas, a single site is the only source of connectivity.
  • Power Equipment Theft at Base Stations: Stolen generator equipment and backup batteries leave base stations completely dependent on an unreliable national electricity grid. Power failures go undetected until the site goes completely dark.
  • No Real-Time Visibility into Site Health: There is no cloud-connected system that can see a fibre signal dropping or backup power discharging and immediately alert tech crews. Everything depends on delayed customer complaints.
Statement of Work — Section 5

SOW-05: Business Impact

5.1 Revenue Loss
Every hour a base station is offline represents direct lost revenue from subscribers who cannot utilize call or data credits. Lagos-based operators lost an estimated N5 billion to infrastructure damage in 2024 alone.

5.2 Regulatory Penalties
The NCC enforces strict QoS standards. In October 2025, Airtel was fined N45 million for QoS service violations alongside two competitors. In May 2025, Airtel was also fined N104 million for SIM registration violations in Kano State. Faster fault detection reduces outages and mitigates regulatory penalty exposures completely.

5.3 Subscriber Churn
Because number portability allows seamless switching, a customer repeatedly experiencing outages will move directly to a competitor. Faster restoration is a pillar of subscriber retention.

Statement of Work — Section 6

SOW-06: Proposed AWS Solution

We propose a cloud-based monitoring and automated response platform built on six AWS services, designed around the robust AWS Well-Architected Framework:

AWS IoT Core Receives continuous readings on fibre loops, battery voltages, and fuel gauges. Uses cellular connections independent of the monitored fibre link, keeping telemetry active even when the physical fibre is completely cut.
Amazon CloudWatch Continuously monitors telemetry streams; raises an alert the moment a reading is out-of-bounds without human delay, powering clean status dashboards for the NOC.
AWS Lambda Runs serverless response logic, classifying types of faults based on incoming indicators and pulling responsible zone details instantly.
Amazon DynamoDB A durable NoSQL registry of base stations and a historic log of every incident, which serves as a clean compliance audit trail for the NCC.
Amazon SNS Evaluates alert severity and sends instant, targeted text/SMS alerts to local zone field crews and dashboards automatically.
Statement of Work — Section 7

SOW-07: Architecture Overview

Step-by-Step Data Flow Architecture:

Step 1: Incident Occurs Fibre cable severed, fuel drops, or cabin is vandalized. Readings are registered instantly by the monitoring device.

Step 2: IoT Core Ingress Device transmits readings via out-of-band mobile data, bypassing local terrestrial fibre links.

Step 3: CloudWatch Monitoring Automatically raises alarms on threshold breaches without human intervention.

Step 4: Lambda Response Code Runs serverless incident analysis, classifying faults and pulling mapping data.

Step 5: DynamoDB Logging Creates a durable NoSQL incident record capturing exact detection timing metrics.

Step 6: SNS Delivery Hits assigned area crew mobile terminals with detailed maps and parameters in seconds.

Step 7: AWS IAM & CloudTrail Security Least-privilege configuration runs throughout, logging activities securely.

Statement of Work — Section 8

SOW-08: Implementation Approach

We propose a 4-Phase rollout schedule constructed for low risk and continuous validation:

Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-2) Configures the security credentials, database tables in DynamoDB (site registration metadata, incident tracking structures), CloudWatch alarms, and baseline system permissions. No hardware is deployed.
Phase 2: Pilot Deployment (Months 3-4) Deployment across 50 base stations in Lagos Mainland West (including Alimosho and Mainland), identified as high-theft corridors (BusinessDay NG, 2025). Gathers real incident data to align thresholds.
Phase 3: Refinement (Months 5-6) Adjusts alarm profiles, reduces false anomalies in CloudWatch, updates routing logic in Lambda, and incorporates reporting dashboards in DynamoDB analytics.
Phase 4: National Rollout (Month 7 Onwards) Scales deployment based on incident frequency prioritizations. Targets fault detection to field team alert delivery within two minutes.
Statement of Work — Section 9

SOW-09: Security Considerations

9.1 Access Control: Services utilize fine-grained IAM roles to maintain least-privilege constraints. Humans accessing dashboards require mandatory multi-factor Cognito authentication.

9.2 Data Protection: All records in DynamoDB are encrypted at rest with AWS-managed keys. Payloads traveling across services use TLS 1.3 encryption in-transit.

9.3 Audit Trail: AWS CloudTrail records every user and service action, producing immutable system histories to support incident reviews and corporate QoS validation.

9.4 NDPR Compliance: Adheres to the Nigeria Data Protection Regulation (NDPR) constraints regarding storage, encryption, and limited retention of personal or operator information.

Statement of Work — Section 10

SOW-10: Reliability and Scalability

10.1 Multi-Incident Parallelism
Organised theft or extreme regional weather can knock out several sites in parallel. Under stress, serverless AWS Lambda isolates individual alarms, spawning parallel processing runs instantly to guarantee equal performance under load.

10.2 Global Availability
DynamoDB replicates records across multiple geographic physical data centers. Point-in-time recovery protects logs from administrative mishaps up to 35 days.

10.3 Platform High Availability
Selected services maintain AWS Service Level Agreements guaranteeing 99.9% uptime or better, certifying that the monitor system remains alive even if physical segments go dark.

Statement of Work — Section 13

SOW-13: Limitations & Assumptions

13.1 Limitations:
- Focuses purely on detection and alert gaps. Physical vandalism cannot be prevented; it requires law enforcement coordination.
- Assumes the site has access to a mobilised network cellular connection independent of the local fiber link.
- Physical sensor placement across Airtel’s estate represents a separate logistics and capital project.

13.2 Assumptions:
- Compatible out-of-band cellular sensor modems are available to transmit messages to AWS IoT Core.
- Field crews possess mobile devices for SMS notifications.
- Independent mobile data feeds are stable at 80% of monitored nodes.

Statement of Work — Section 14

SOW-14: Project Conclusion

Nigeria's digital economy is growing faster than the physical infrastructure supporting it can be protected. Airtel Nigeria, serving over 57 million subscribers on a network that sustains more than 43 documented fibre cuts per day, operates in an environment where the response to physical infrastructure failure determines how much of that damage subscribers actually experience as lost service.

This proposed solution does not solve vandalism. What it does is ensure that Airtel finds out about physical infrastructure failures within seconds rather than hours. By connecting base station sensors to cloud-based monitoring through AWS IoT Core, detecting anomalies automatically through Amazon CloudWatch, running response logic through AWS Lambda, storing incident records in Amazon DynamoDB, and delivering targeted alerts through Amazon SNS, the platform turns a reactive model that depends on subscriber complaints into an automated one that acts before subscribers notice anything is wrong.
